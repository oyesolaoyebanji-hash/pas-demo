Field 01 of 07
The Artifact
Required
What is this, exactly
Name the artifact with precision. Not "a document" — a client-facing capability statement. Not "a page" — a procurement portal landing screen. Not "an email" — a cold outreach to an embassy procurement officer.
What it must do
One sentence. Not what it contains — what it achieves.
Example

"A three-page capability statement. Its job: give a procurement officer everything needed to shortlist this vendor without a follow-up meeting."

Field 02 of 07
The Reader
Required
Who reads this
Name the specific person, not a category. State their role, their context, their exposure level — how many similar documents, proposals, or outputs they encounter in a typical month.
What they already know
Everything they already know gets cut from the output. State it so it can be eliminated.
What would make them dismiss this
The one thing that causes them to stop engaging.
Example

"A senior government director. Has reviewed 30 similar submissions this month. Understands the sector. Dismissal trigger: generic claims, poor structure, or wrong register."

Field 03 of 07
The Context
Required
Where does this live
Channel and moment of consumption. Where does the reader encounter this — printed, on screen, on a phone? At what point in the conversation or process does it arrive?
What precedes and follows it
What has the reader already seen or heard? What action should they take immediately after?
Example

"Sent as a PDF attachment after an introductory call. Reader has heard the pitch once. Next action: forward internally for approval or request a second meeting."

Field 04 of 07
The Constraints
Required
Hard limits
Non-negotiable parameters. Length, page count, word count, sections that must exist, information that must appear.
Hard prohibitions
What must not appear. The most powerful field. Name specific phrases, formats, tones, visual elements. Prohibitions break defaults more reliably than instructions.
Existing assets to use
Brand colors, existing copy, data, logo, specific terminology — or state that full creative latitude applies.
Example

"Max two pages. Must include three core sections and a single call to action. No bullet points. No stock phrases. No icons. Brand colors already established."

Field 05 of 07
The Feeling
Required
Three tone words
Exactly three. Choose from: cold / warm / urgent / institutional / quiet / sparse / dense / authoritative / restrained / clinical / editorial / assured / formal / severe / measured / matter-of-fact — or name your own.
The 3-second impression
What should the reader feel before they read a single word? Not what they think — what they feel on first contact.
One reference
A publication, brand, institution, document type, country's design culture, or decade. Be specific and unexpected.
Example

"Tone: assured, spare, institutional. 3-second impression: this is a provider that does not negotiate. Reference: a Zurich private bank's client welcome document."

Field 06 of 07
The Information
Situational
Raw data, facts and copy
Paste the actual data, facts, figures, or copy that must appear. Do not summarize — provide it raw. If none, write "none — full creative latitude."
What to infer vs what to invent
State what can be inferred, what can be invented to fill gaps, and what absolutely cannot be invented.
Example

"All figures and names are real — do not alter. Supporting descriptions can be written from context. Do not invent quotes, statistics, or client references."

Field 07 of 07
The Benchmark
Situational
How you will know it worked
One sentence. A specific, observable outcome — not "it looks good."
What failure looks like
Name the specific failure mode you are most worried about. Naming it activates every prohibition set in Field 04.
Example

"Success: the reader can act on this without asking a single follow-up question. Failure: it reads like every other document in this category."
